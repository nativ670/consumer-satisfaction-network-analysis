Introduction
  • The Multi-Attribute Problem: Traditional models assume satisfaction is a simple sum of independent feature weights3.
  • Cognitive Interdependence: The Halo Effect & Spreading Activation theory tell us that consumers evaluate attributes interactively using compensatory heuristics6,2.
  • Network Paradigm: Reconceptualizing satisfaction as an emergent property of a dynamic interacting attribute network1.
  • The J-Shaped Paradox: Review distributions indicate that consumer evaluation reflects polarized behavioural groups rather than a continuous state4.
Research Objectives
TH

Are consumer evaluations based on independent product attributes or on interacting cognitive schemas?

OP

Does incorporating network interactions improve the modeling of customer satisfaction compared with additive approaches?

Synthesis of Findings

  • Two aspect clusters emerged: External/Logistical and Internal/Sensory.
  • Most interaction coefficients were negative.
  • The ordinal model predominantly predicted extreme ratings (1★ and 5★).
  • Interaction terms improved model fit but did not improve predictive performance in the binary and ordinal framework.

Discussion
  • Satisfaction emerges from conditional interactions between attributes rather than independent contributions - consistent with Gestalt evaluation and Spreading Activation theory.5,2
  • Predominantly negative interactions suggest compensatory trade-offs, where strengths in one aspect can offset weaknesses in another.
  • Consumers evaluate intrinsic quality and extrinsic logistics as distinct cognitive systems, bridged structurally by Packaging and Ingredients.
  • While predictive gains were modest, the network substantially improved model fit, suggesting its primary contribution is explanatory rather than predictive.
