Introduction & Background
  • The Problem: Traditional consumer behavior models assume product attributes (e.g., price, quality) independently sum to create overall satisfaction.
  • J-Shaped Reality: Amazon reviews (n=322,133) strongly polarize into 1-star and 5-star ratings, indicating complex behavioral heuristics rather than a continuous linear score.
  • Cognitive Trade-offs: Consumers evaluate attributes interactively using compensatory heuristics (e.g., the Halo Effect), where features offset one another.
  • The Solution: Modern network psychometrics reconceptualizes satisfaction as an emergent property of a dynamic interacting network of observable perceptions.
Research Questions

1. Theoretical Question: In the process of forming an opinion, how are different attributes weighted in mental models, and do interactions shape overall satisfaction?

2. Operational Question: Does incorporating measured network interactions (GLASSO) into a predictive model improve accuracy compared to a baseline additive model?

Result Synthesis

Integrating GLASSO-derived network edges universally improved model fit (Adj. R²) while drastically lowering structural complexity (BIC drop of ~6,800). Every single interaction coefficient was negative, mathematically capturing the cognitive compensatory heuristic: overwhelming positive sentiment in one attribute cushions deficiencies in another.

Discussion & Limitations

The finding that interactions are consistently negative serves as a classic indicator of a compensatory psychological mechanism (diminishing marginal utility). If a product is outstanding in "Ingredients", consumers mentally forgive minor flaws in "Price" or "Packaging".

Limitation: A potential limitation of using OLS regression on a bounded 1-5 star scale is the "Ceiling Effect," potentially forcing negative interactions. However, by validating this structure across Ordinal and Binary frameworks (which gracefully handle discrete boundaries), we proved this is a genuine cognitive structure, not a statistical artifact.
